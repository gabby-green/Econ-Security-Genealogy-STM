It is a pleasure to join you today to open this timely event, hosted by the European Policy Centre. With such an impressive line-up of speakers, I am sure that we are in for a rich and insightful experience.

Conversations like these help us to see current issues through fresh perspectives and shape effective policy responses. I have no doubt today will be no different, especially given the extraordinary context we face in trade and economic security – topics I am here to address.

*

We are witnessing a profound paradigm shift in global trade and in how we define economic strength and security. The romantic “good old days” of trade – when the environment was simpler, relationships less complex, and balances more predictable – are firmly behind us.

But that does not mean that we should give up on our values, our goals, or our determination.

On the contrary. We need trade.

Trade is not just a political slogan. It is an economic imperative.

The World Bank estimates that approximately one billion people have been lifted out of extreme poverty over the past three decades. Many countries once classified as least developed, are now emerging players – industrialising, innovating, and steadily joining the global middle class.

This has not been achieved through protectionism.

Trade is progress. Trade is peace in motion.

Moreover, every product we use tells a story of global interdependence.

Your car may be designed in Germany, manufactured in Slovakia, equipped with semiconductors from Taiwan, steel from the U.S., and – if electric – lithium sourced from Chile or batteries from China.

A single smartphone also relies on components sourced worldwide, involving dozens of countries – for example, processors from the U.S., displays from Japan, batteries from South Korea, or camera components from Germany.

This is the reality of today's supply chains – complex, collaborative, interdependent.

Thanks to these global interconnections, we better understand each other and the challenges we face – from the growing impacts of climate change, like wildfires and floods, to the AI revolution.

*

The European Union has always stood for free and fair trade. Robert Schuman emphasised this when he said: “Europe cannot be made without making economic progress, and economic progress depends on free trade and cooperation.”

So, we negotiate. We do not isolate. We do not leave the table.

This is reflected in our network of trade agreements, the largest of any trading player worldwide – 44 deals with 76 countries, covering nearly half of our global trade.

There's nothing abstract about it. These agreements remove barriers, attract investment, and open new markets, benefitting everyone involved.

For us, trade agreements are more than transactions. They are upgrades that empower our partners, helping them grow with us and creating a cycle of shared prosperity.

I am confident in saying that every free trade agreement we have negotiated has boosted growth, jobs, and markets.

Therefore, in the current geopolitical context, we are doubling down on a rules-based approach to trade, fast-tracking bilateral free trade agreement talks already underway, and initiating numerous new ones.

Our focus is on reaching commercially meaningful, mutually beneficial deals tailored to each partner.

Given the paradigm shift in the global trade landscape, we are going beyond traditional FTAs to include digital agreements, sustainable investment facilitation, and Clean Trade and Investment Partnerships, among others.

I value every discussion with business leaders. So, we spare no time and effort in opening new markets, while strengthening the EU's position in global trade and the rules-based system for the long haul.

The message is clear: the EU is open for business, reliable, and committed to fairness and mutual gain.

At the same time, we are making Europe a more attractive place to invest, by boosting competitiveness and simplifying both our laws and internal processes.

For too long, the EU has outsourced production in critical sectors. We built over-dependencies where we should have been building resilience. We have learned – sometimes the hard way – that such overdependence can be weaponised.

That is why I see our Single Market and our trade and investment partnerships as two sides of the same coin.

But let's be clear: free trade does not mean naïve trade.

The global system has been disrupted by non-market practices fuelling industrial overcapacity, by stalled multilateral rulemaking, and even by the weaponisation of supply chains.

To be honest, we have been playing catch-up. The pace of geopolitical and economic shifts demand that we adapt – and fast.

*

Ladies and gentlemen,

We are here to improve the system – not to bend it to the point of breaking, and certainly not to abandon it.

But maintaining the status quo is a non-starter.

Just in the past two days, during the OECD Trade Ministerial, the message was clear and unequivocal – deep reform of the World Trade Organisation is long overdue and urgently needed to match today's realities.

We need fresh perspectives on multiple fronts.

And that includes China, our second-largest trading partner in goods, with whom the EU is celebrating the 50th anniversary of diplomatic relations.

As a major global player, China has a responsibility to match its support for multilateralism with full respect for the rules meant to ensure fairness and equity.

Hence, our calls on China to address systemic issues affecting Europe and other partners, such as overcapacity, subsidies, market access barriers, critical minerals export restrictions, investment conditions, and trade diversion.

The EU and China share strong mutual interests in tackling both bilateral and global challenges. But it is equally important to address our differences, as the quality of our cooperation impacts not only our industries and our people, but also the entire global economy.

We value the EU-China economic and trade relationship. However, China's impressive rise must not come at the expense of the European economy.

We are committed to rebalancing this relationship and establishing a level playing field in trade and investment, with symmetrical market opening.

Earlier this week, during the OECD Ministerial Council, I held yet another focused, in-depth discussion with Minister Wang Wentao on a wide range of urgent issues vital to European industry – as I have consistently done since the beginning of my mandate.

We are working hard to drive progress ahead of the leaders' summit in July, which I trust will create fresh momentum to reshape our economic and trade relations.

*

Ladies and gentlemen,

For decades, the United States – our largest trading partner – led by example on rules-based trade. As President Ronald Reagan once said: “We should be leading the world in opening markets, not retreating behind walls.”

We are currently engaged in complex negotiations with the U.S., so please understand that I cannot go into too much detail today.

However, it is important to emphasise that EU-U.S. relations are unparalleled and must be approached with mutual respect and a strategic vision.

While we share common goals – especially around reindustrialisation and leadership in future-oriented sectors – we have chosen different paths.

The U.S. favours tariffs and reshoring. The EU sees value in diversification via reliable partnerships around the globe.

From day one, the Commission has been in constant dialogue with the U.S. I have spoken numerous times with my counterparts, Secretary Howard Lutnick and Ambassador Jamieson Greer.

Just yesterday, I had a productive meeting with the U.S. Trade Representative in Paris, while our experts are spending this week in Washington.

So, we are fully investing our time and effort, at all levels.

And I am glad to say that our engagement has been very constructive, and we are moving in the right direction – and I am committed to sustaining this positive momentum moving forward.

We face many shared challenges, and we are better off tackling them together.

*

Ladies and gentlemen,

This brings me to economic security – no longer an abstract concept, but a pressing priority that demands immediate action.

Today's event is clear proof of this urgency, as is the EU's rapid progress.

Our objective is straightforward – to identify real, high-risk vulnerabilities across critical areas and act swiftly to address them, starting with an assessment of the tools at our disposal.

When it comes to new technologies, for instance, we are focused on four sectors that will shape the future: advanced semiconductors, artificial intelligence, quantum technologies, and biotechnology. We are moving fast and expect the first set of results in the coming weeks.

In parallel, we are preparing to launch assessments on six additional emerging technologies, accelerating our pace to stay ahead of evolving threats.

But assessments are only the starting point.

Mitigation is front and centre here.

This means deploying – and where needed, reinforcing – our toolbox, from foreign direct investment screening to export controls, monitoring of outbound investments, the foreign subsidies regulation, and the anti-coercion instrument.

This work demands close coordination with our Member States and industry partners.

And because economic security does not end at our borders, we are deepening cooperation with like-minded partners, including Japan, Canada, Australia, and within the G7 – for instance, to establish standards for resilient supply chains to support long-term economic security.

It is essential that we build on our many strengths. Supporting companies that are indispensable to global value chains and doubling down on our R&D capabilities must remain top priorities.

I am convinced that all this will keep us one step ahead – focused, aligned, responsive.

And to protect our Single Market, we must modernise how we manage trade at our borders.

That is where the bold reform of our Customs Union comes in, with the creation of the EU Customs Authority and the Customs Data Hub.

These will be game changers, offering immediate and tangible benefits – simpler processes, better data, and stronger protection for both Europeans and European businesses.

So, economic security is no longer an optional extra.

It is mission critical.

And Europe is stepping up.

*

In conclusion, I will borrow the words of Winston Churchill, who once said,

“To each, there comes in their lifetime a special moment when they are figuratively tapped on the shoulder and offered the chance to do a very special thing, unique to them and fit for their talents.”

Now is that moment for Europe.

Let's build a system that is open, reliable, and resilient. Let's break down silos and avoid putting up walls, while resolutely championing free and fair trade.

And putting on my institutional hat, let's do this united – as one strong Europe.

Thank you.
